Healthcare facilities — hospitals, long-term care homes (LTCs), specialized clinics — are among the most demanding environments for wall protection systems. Between hygiene constraints, repeated impacts from medical equipment, and accessibility requirements, every product decision must be carefully considered.

## Why Wall Protection Is Critical in Healthcare

In an LTC corridor, a motorized hospital bed or electric wheelchair can weigh over 200 kg. At low speed, an impact against an unprotected wall generates enough contact pressure to damage finishes — and in some cases, the structure itself.

Repeated damage has several consequences:

- **High repair costs**: repainting a hospital corridor can cost thousands of dollars due to aseptic requirements
- **Infection risks**: damaged surfaces create crevices where bacteria can accumulate
- **Brand image**: a facility with damaged walls projects a negative image to patients and families

## Applicable Standards in Healthcare Settings

### Hygiene and Decontamination (CSA Z8000)

The Canadian standard **CSA Z8000** for healthcare facilities requires that all surfaces be washable, non-porous, and resistant to common disinfectants (including sodium hypochlorite and hydrogen peroxide).

Wall protection must therefore:
- Withstand daily cleaning cycles with hospital-grade disinfectants
- Have no open joints where microorganisms could multiply
- Be PVC-free in new construction (preference for polyethylene or stainless steel)

### Accessibility and Handrails (CCQ Art. 3.8)

Any facility serving people with reduced mobility must equip its corridors with continuous handrails on at least one side. These handrails must:

- Be of contrasting color from the wall (luminosity ratio ≥ 70%)
- Have a diameter between **32 mm and 50 mm**
- Be installed at **865-965 mm** from the floor

## Kleston Solutions for Healthcare

### Corner and Edge Protectors

Right angles are the most vulnerable zones. Our corner protectors in anodized aluminum or 316L stainless steel can be installed for the full wall height. They withstand lateral impacts up to 800 J (joules) per EN 14876 testing.

### Wall Bumper Rail

The bumper rail is installed at chair-back height (approximately 900 mm) and armrest height (approximately 600 mm). It forms a continuous barrier that absorbs impacts from medical equipment.

Our bumper rails are available in:

## Installation Process in Active Facilities

Installation in an operating healthcare facility requires careful planning:

1. **Sectoral work**: intervention by wing or corridor, never simultaneous across the entire facility
2. **Adapted schedules**: work performed outside peak traffic hours (often nights or weekends)
3. **Temporary zoning**: construction barriers compliant with infection control protocols
4. **Certified cleanup**: surface decontamination at the end of each workday

## Return on Investment

Investment in quality wall protection systems is typically recovered within 2 to 4 years in most healthcare settings. This calculation accounts for:

- Reduced painting and repair costs
- Lower risk of incidents related to damaged surfaces
- Improved satisfaction among residents and staff
